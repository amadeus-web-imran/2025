## Response:
Intergenerational trauma, also known as transgenerational trauma, is the transmission of trauma and its effects from one generation to the next. It's not just the memory of a traumatic event that's passed down, but the emotional, psychological, and even physiological impact. It's a key concept in understanding why families can feel stuck in unhealthy cycles of behavior.

### How it Works

The concept originated from studies on the children of Holocaust survivors, who showed symptoms of PTSD despite not having directly experienced the trauma themselves. Since then, it's been studied in the descendants of various groups who have endured collective trauma, such as indigenous populations, war veterans, and those affected by systemic oppression.

Intergenerational trauma is passed down in three main ways:

*   **Behavioral:** Parents who have experienced trauma may struggle with emotional regulation, attachment, and healthy communication. This can lead to a chaotic home environment, a lack of emotional support, or a tendency to resort to anger or avoidance. Children, in turn, may internalize these behaviors as normal and carry them into their own lives and relationships.
*   **Psychological:** The psychological effects of trauma can manifest as anxiety, depression, and a general sense of being unsafe in the world. This can make it difficult for parents to nurture their children's emotional well-being, leading to a sense of anxiety or insecurity in the child.
*   **Biological:** Emerging research in epigenetics suggests that trauma can alter gene expression, meaning that the effects of a parent's trauma can be passed down biologically, making the next generation more susceptible to stress and mental health issues. This is a potential way in which the body "remembers" trauma even when the mind does not.

### Sakhi's Approach

Sakhi's work directly addresses intergenerational trauma by helping people become aware of these patterns. She would not blame the parents or the ancestors, but would instead help the individual recognize their own "unhealed wounds" as a part of a larger, intergenerational story. By consciously choosing to heal and to communicate differently, a person can stop the cycle of trauma and create a healthier foundation for their own family. This is the ultimate act of "emotional and spiritual cleansing."
